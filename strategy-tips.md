# The definitive strategy guide to Lost Cities

**Lost Cities rewards disciplined restraint far more than ambitious exploration.** The central tension of Reiner Knizia's elegant two-player card game is that every expedition you start costs you 20 points before a single number card is played — and wager cards amplify that risk. The players who win consistently aren't those who chase all five colors; they're the ones who commit hard to three, read the discard piles like open books, and know the exact mathematical thresholds that separate profitable expeditions from costly mistakes. This guide distills the best strategic thinking from top-ranked competitive players into a single reference.

---

## How the game works in 60 seconds

Lost Cities features **60 cards** across five expedition colors (yellow, blue, white, green, red), each containing nine number cards (2–10) and three investment/wager cards (the "handshake" cards). Players alternate turns: play one card to an expedition or discard it, then draw one card from either the draw pile or the top of any discard pile. Cards in an expedition must be played in ascending order, and wager cards must precede all number cards. The game ends instantly when the draw pile is exhausted.

Scoring per expedition follows this formula: **(sum of face values − 20) × (1 + number of wager cards) + 20 bonus if ≥ 8 cards**. An unstarted expedition scores zero. The 20-point bonus is **not** multiplied by wagers. Each expedition can score anywhere from **−80 points** (three wagers, no number cards) to **+156 points** (three wagers plus all nine number cards with the 8-card bonus). Each player starts with 8 cards, the draw pile holds 44, and each player gets roughly **22 turns** before the deck runs out.

## The golden rule: commit to three colors, sometimes four, never five

The single most important strategic principle in Lost Cities is expedition selection. Every source — from BoardGameGeek tournament veterans to Board Game Arena's top-ranked players — converges on the same advice: **target three expeditions per round**. You'll frequently end up with four, but five is almost always a mistake.

The math explains why. With roughly 22 turns per player and 8 cards stranded in your hand at game's end, you can place around **14 cards onto expeditions** (the rest go to discards). Spreading those across five colors means fewer than three cards per expedition, and three low-to-mid cards rarely reach the 20-point breakeven threshold. Meanwhile, concentrating on three colors lets you average **5–7 cards per expedition**, making profitable scores routine and the 8-card bonus achievable.

There are two viable strategic archetypes. The first is the "home run" approach: invest heavily in one or two colors with multiple wager cards, aiming for massive scores of 80–120 points per expedition. The second is the "base hits" approach: score modest 5–15 points across four expeditions without wagers. Most experienced players blend these — one or two power expeditions supported by a couple of safe, moderate ones. The key exception to the three-color rule arises when you hold three high cards (like 8-9-10) in a fourth or fifth color. Playing those three cards yields a face sum of 27, netting **+7 points** with minimal risk and only three turns invested.

## Breakeven is always 20, and wagers are symmetric amplifiers

The most misunderstood aspect of Lost Cities scoring is how wager cards interact with the 20-point expedition cost. **The breakeven face-value threshold is always 20, regardless of how many wager cards you play.** Wagers multiply the deviation from 20 in both directions — they don't change where the break-even point sits.

| Face value sum | 0 wagers (×1) | 1 wager (×2) | 2 wagers (×3) | 3 wagers (×4) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 10 | −10 | −20 | −30 | −40 |
| 15 | −5 | −10 | −15 | −20 |
| **20** | **0** | **0** | **0** | **0** |
| 25 | +5 | +10 | +15 | +20 |
| 30 | +10 | +20 | +30 | +40 |
| 40 | +20 | +40 | +60 | +80 |
| 54 (max) | +34 | +68 | +102 | +136 |

This means wager cards are not inherently risky — they're risky only when you're uncertain whether your face sum will exceed 20. If you're confident of hitting 25+ face value, every wager is pure upside. The real risk lies in the uncertainty of future draws. A useful decision rule: if the probability your face sum exceeds 20 is greater than **50%**, a single wager is positive expected value. If you're **80%+ confident** of reaching 25, multiple wagers become strongly profitable.

You always need **at least three number cards** to break even, and those three cards must average at least 6.67 each. The highest three-card combination (8+9+10 = 27) comfortably clears the threshold; the lowest viable three-card set requires at least one card of value 8 or higher (e.g., 4+8+10 = 22, or 5+7+8 = 20).

## When to start an expedition: the one-card rule

Top-ranked player Marc Davis (peaked 13th on Board Game Arena) offers the best decision heuristic for expedition commitment: **"If you play the number cards you currently hold in this color and draw only one more playable card, will you break even?"**

Consider holding two wager cards plus the 5 and 7 of yellow (face sum = 12). You need 8 more face-value points to reach 20. Drawing any single card valued 8, 9, or 10 gets you there. With three qualifying cards potentially available and the game still young, the probability of drawing at least one is roughly **82%**, even accounting for the ~40% chance your opponent holds one in their starting hand. This makes the expedition a strong commitment.

Contrast this with holding only a lone 10 in some color. Your face sum is 10, leaving you 10 points short of breakeven. You'd need to draw two more cards averaging 5+ each — possible but unreliable, especially mid-game. Starting this expedition without additional cards in hand is usually a mistake.

**Timing thresholds for starting expeditions refine this further.** In the early game (first third of the draw pile), playing a wager card with few number cards is acceptable — statistically, about 27 points' worth of number cards in each color will pass through your hands over a full game. By mid-game (around one-third through), don't start a wagered expedition unless you already hold **at least 10 face-value points** of number cards in that color. In the late game, only start new expeditions if you can immediately play enough high cards to profit.

## Wager cards deserve early, strategic deployment

Wager cards must precede all number cards in their expedition, creating an acute timing pressure: every turn you hold a wager is a turn you might need to start playing numbers. **Play wager cards as early as possible** once you've decided to commit to a color.

A counterintuitive but well-supported tactic involves the order in which you play wagers across colors. When you hold wagers in multiple colors, **play the wager in your weakest color first**. This conceals your strongest suit and may bait your opponent into discarding valuable cards in that hidden power color. Playing the wager in your best color first advertises your intentions and lets your opponent adjust.

The 8-card bonus transforms wager card economics dramatically. The 20-point bonus requires 8 total cards in one expedition. With three wagers already occupying three slots, you need only **5 number cards** to trigger the bonus — compared to 8 of 9 possible number cards without any wagers (nearly impossible). This makes the 3-wager path the most accessible route to the bonus. A realistic high-wager scenario: 3 wagers + cards 6+7+8+9+10 (face sum = 40) scores (40−20)×4 + 20 = **100 points**, which is typically game-winning by itself.

| Wagers played | Number cards needed for 8-card bonus | Difficulty |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | 5 of 9 | Achievable with focus |
| 2 | 6 of 9 | Difficult but realistic |
| 1 | 7 of 9 | Very difficult |
| 0 | 8 of 9 | Nearly impossible |

## Card counting separates competent players from strong ones

Lost Cities has unusually high open information for a card game. You can always see your 8-card hand, all played expedition cards for both players, and the top card of each discard pile. Crucially, you were present when every discard was made — **the only hidden information is your opponent's hand and the draw pile contents**, and both shrink as the game progresses.

For each color, track the 12 total cards (3 wagers + numbers 2–10). Subtract everything visible: your hand, both players' expedition cards, and cards you've seen discarded. The remainder tells you what's still in the draw pile or your opponent's hand. If you've accounted for 8 of 12 green cards, only 4 remain unknown — and any specific card you need has a calculable probability of appearing. With 8 unknown cards in your opponent's hand out of roughly 52 unseen cards early in the game, the chance they hold any specific card is about **15.4%**. For three specific cards (say the 8, 9, and 10 of yellow), the probability they hold at least one is approximately **40%**.

**Watch for these signals from your opponent**: drawing from a discard pile reveals interest in that color; discarding a high card signals abandonment of that color; discarding a wager card means that color's multiplier potential is shrinking. Track whether wagers in each color have surfaced — if zero wagers of a color have appeared anywhere, all three are lurking in the draw pile or your opponent's hand, which has major implications for both players' potential in that color.

## Discard pile management is where games are won and lost

The discard decision is the game's most consequential and most frequently botched calculation. Before discarding any card, compute its **effective value to your opponent**: face value × (opponent's wager cards in that color + 1). A humble 3 in a color where your opponent has two wagers is worth 3 × 3 = **9 points to them** — more damaging than discarding an 8 in an unwagered color (worth just 8). Always run this multiplication before every discard.

**Safe discards** include cards valued 5 or below in colors your opponent hasn't started, cards below your opponent's lowest played card in any color (they can't play them in ascending order), and wager cards in colors where both players have already played number cards. **Dangerous discards** are any playable card in a color where your opponent has active wager multipliers and high cards in colors your opponent is visibly collecting.

**Drawing from discard piles versus the draw pile** carries strategic weight beyond the card itself. Drawing from the draw pile depletes it, bringing the game closer to ending. Drawing from a discard pile preserves the draw pile, effectively extending the game. **Default to drawing from the draw pile** — discard pile cards tend to be low-value (that's why they were discarded), and drawing from the deck preserves hidden information about your strategy. Skip discard pile cards worth 4 or less unless they feed a heavily wagered expedition. The key exception: draw from discard piles to extend the game when you still have strong cards to play and need more turns.

A subtle advanced tactic: you can recover your own previously discarded cards. If you discard a low card early as a bluff and circumstances change, you can pick it back up from the discard pile on a later turn (just not the same turn you discarded it).

## Controlling the clock determines who wins close games

Game length is not fixed — it's a contested resource. The draw pile starts at 44 cards, and **turns remaining equals roughly half the draw pile** (since both players draw each turn). At 18 cards remaining, you don't have 18 turns — you have about **9**. This catches beginners off guard constantly.

**When you're ahead, accelerate.** Draw exclusively from the draw pile to end the game before your opponent completes their expeditions. If they've committed wagers but haven't played enough number cards, a premature ending traps them with devastating negative multiplied scores. **When you're behind, extend.** Draw from discard piles to buy turns, even picking up marginally useful cards. Go aggressive on one or two colors, playing high-risk wager strategies because conservative play guarantees a loss.

A useful mental checkpoint: at **16 cards remaining** in the draw pile, you can still play every card in your hand if you draw from the pile each turn. At **8 cards remaining**, you can only play your full hand if you draw exclusively from discard piles. Plan your endgame sequence accordingly — if you're holding cards you need to play, count backward from the expected game end to ensure you have enough turns.

## Eight mistakes that cost beginners the most points

The most damaging beginner error is **spreading across all five colors**, turning every expedition into an underfunded liability. Close behind is **holding wager cards too long** waiting for certainty — by the time you're sure, it's too late to play the wager before your number cards. Third is **failing to count the draw pile**, which leads to playing wagers in the final third of the game with no time to recoup the investment.

Other common mistakes include waiting for perfect sequences (skipping a gap costs one card but saves critical tempo), ignoring opponent board state (this is not solitaire — blocking matters as much as building), carelessly discarding into opponent's wagered colors, underestimating how wagers amplify losses (not just gains), and treating the game as a race for cards rather than a race for **points**. A targeted 3-card expedition scoring +7 is worth more than a sprawling 6-card expedition scoring +2.

## Advanced tactics for reading and deceiving your opponent

**The low-card bait** works roughly 20% of the time in competitive online play. If you hold strong cards in a color but no wagers (say 5, 6, 7, 9 of blue), discard the lowest card to signal weakness. Your opponent may feel safe discarding a blue wager card, which you immediately scoop up. The risk is minimal since low cards contribute little face value.

**Pressure through commitment ambiguity** is more consistently effective. If you've played a wager in white but no number cards yet, your opponent must hold onto any white cards they have — discarding them risks feeding your multiplied expedition. This clogs their hand and forces suboptimal discards elsewhere. The longer you delay playing numbers after a wager, the more hand-space pressure you exert.

**"Dump color" expeditions** are a deliberate advanced tactic, not a mistake. Playing 3+4+5 in a throwaway color costs only **−8 points** but buys three turns of draws from the deck while you hunt for cards in your power color. The expected loss is small, and there's meaningful upside: you'll likely draw at least one more card in that dump color, potentially improving it to breakeven or better. Think of −8 points as an investment in three lottery tickets from the draw pile.

**Playing from behind** requires a fundamental mindset shift. Abandon safe plays entirely. Go all-in on one or two colors with maximum wagers, because the variance that makes wagers "risky" is exactly what you need — conservative play merely guarantees a slow loss. Conversely, **playing from ahead** means minimizing variance: avoid new expeditions, draw from the deck to end the game, take sure points over speculative ones.

## Conclusion: the real game beneath the cards

Lost Cities appears to be about exploring exotic locations, but it's actually a game of **resource allocation under uncertainty** — a compressed negotiation between ambition and arithmetic. The players who win consistently internalize three principles. First, the 20-point breakeven threshold governs everything; never start an expedition without a credible path to clearing it. Second, information is the scarcest resource — card counting, opponent-reading, and discard-pile awareness create more edge than any single lucky draw. Third, the game clock is not a passive countdown but an active weapon: whoever controls tempo controls the outcome. Master these, and Lost Cities transforms from a light filler into a deeply rewarding strategic duel.
